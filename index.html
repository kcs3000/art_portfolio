Our Melting Earth

I was inspired by my love of the ocean, climate change research, photos and artwork about the changing climate, snorkeling in Hawaii, videos by student activists, and David Attenborough documentaries. Most important to me is my concern about what the planet will be like when I'm older. My art reflects the sense of urgency I feel. I want to show people the dangers of climate change and tell them that they can make a difference. There is so much uncertainty about how climate change will affect my life, and it is hard to understand.

The immediate impact of my art is stark and very visual-- representing the changes that are happening right now and also showing how bad things could get if no one does anything. One person can make a difference, but together we can make a big difference and create a better environmental future for us all.

Water is a huge concern. Most of the water in the world is not fresh and much of the freshwater is water we can't even access. There is a finite amount of freshwater no matter how long the earth survives-- unfortunately, that is all of the freshwater there will be.

Even though I'm very young, I feel the effects of climate change. Summers feel warmer and drier and if this continues, things will get a lot hotter here. I want to do my part; some of the changes that I can personally make include: using less plastic; sharing my artwork and the climate change message with my classmates, trying to carpool and being conscientious about gas usage. I'm already careful about how much water I use around the house and will continue to do that. I feel that art is an interesting way to express thoughts and share ideas about climate change. I also hope more discussions about climate change will motivate more people to help solve this life-altering problem.

This piece was submitted to Bow Seat Ocean Awareness Program Art Contest 2021 theme - WATER RISING.

What will happen to the polar bears?

The idea was to create a piece of art that described a future world. My theme was related to climate change and its potential effect on polar bears. I had recently read an article about declining polar bear populations and how their habitat was being threatened by climate change. This project allowed me to see art in a new way; as a way to communicate an idea in a very different form from an article or speech. I also saw how art could produce a different emotional response than other forms of communication. My piece on the polar bears along with other winning contestants was exhibited at the Peninsula art museum.

This piece won the 2nd place of the Arts and Future Contest 2019 theme - OUR WORLD OUR FUTURE.

Penguin Pool Party

I was inspired by my love of the ocean, climate change research, photos and artwork about the changing climate, snorkeling in Hawaii, videos by student activists, and David Attenborough documentaries. Visual art such as mine can send a message without words, much like an editorial cartoon. I like to express my feelings this way. I decided to draw penguins because they represent humor in nature to me. Penguins appear comical no matter what they’re doing.

In this drawing, they’re swimming in a pool because the ocean is too polluted and has too much plastic. Wouldn't you like to have penguins in your pool?  At the same time, wouldn’t you be horrified to know why they were there? It’s a fact that if you can get people to laugh or smile at something, it’s more possible for them to see the message in the humor. The message here is hope - hope that people and governments can make enough changes to save this downward slide in our climate.

I've been following climate change research for a few years now, and the news keeps getting worse and worse. No wonder the subject this year is humor. More than ever, I'm worried about what the planet will be like when I grow up. My middle school has a program for us called the 'Environmental Ambassador Committee' which meets bi-weekly and I'm a member of it. Together we talk about what we can do to help make a difference with climate change and the environment. In April, we planned Earth Day activities, and we also are coordinating with the TerraCycling program, a company that encourages recycling of hard-to-recycle materials. San Francisco is a very progressive city when it comes to climate change activities. The city has already reduced its greenhouse gas emissions 36 percent below 1990 levels. Their laws will reduce emissions from the City's largest commercial buildings by an additional 21 percent to accelerate San Francisco's drive towards 100 percent renewable electricity by 2030. (San Francisco – CNCA - Carbon Neutral Cities Alliance)

I am hopeful that I can use my talents to help to spread the message about climate change. I also want my art to motivate people to do their part in helping to solve these immensely life-altering problems.

This piece was submitted to Bow Seat Ocean Awareness Program Art Contest 2022 theme - THE FUNNY THING ABOUT CLIMATE CHANGE.

Water is Precious

The state of California has been experiencing a drought, yet it continues to endure. This piece shows a parched California alongside some of my favorite coastal elements. When I think of the potential consequences of a persistent drought, I am saddened by its disruptive effects. Both animal life and California landmarks could disappear. Everything in California could be threatened by drought if we don’t learn how to better manage our water resources.

This piece was submitted to California Coastal Art & Poetry Contest 2023.
